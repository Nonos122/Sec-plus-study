## Data Types

1. PII (Personally Identifiable Information) — data that can identify a specific individual (name, SSN, address, email).
2. PHI (Protected Health Information) — health-related data tied to an individual, protected under regulations like HIPAA.
3. Financial data — banking details, transaction records, credit card numbers, and other monetary information.
4. Legal data — contracts, case files, and other documents tied to legal proceedings or obligations.
5. IP (Intellectual Property) — creations of the mind (patents, designs, processes) that an organization has legal rights to.
6. Consumer data — information collected about customers, such as purchase history or preferences.
7. Government data — data owned or classified by a government entity, often subject to strict handling rules.
8. Trade secrets — confidential business information (formulas, methods, processes) that provides a competitive advantage.
9. Customer data — information a business holds about the people it serves (contact details, account history).
10. Proprietary data — data owned exclusively by an organization, not intended for public release.
11. Biometric data — physical characteristics (fingerprint, facial scan, iris) used to identify an individual.
12. Distinctions:
    - Human-readable — data in a format people can directly read and understand (e.g., plaintext documents, printed reports).
    - Non-human-readable — data in a format meant for machine processing (e.g., binary, encrypted ciphertext, compiled code).

### Data Privacy & Protection Regulations

| Regulation | Jurisdiction / Scope | Data Protected | Key Requirement |
|---|---|---|---|
| GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation) | European Union (applies to any org handling EU residents' data) | Personal data of EU residents | Requires consent for data collection, breach notification within 72 hours, and the right to be forgotten. |
| HIPAA (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) | United States | PHI (Protected Health Information) | Requires safeguards for storing, transmitting, and sharing patient health data. |
| CCPA (California Consumer Privacy Act) | California, USA | Personal data of California residents | Gives consumers the right to know what data is collected and to request deletion or opt out of its sale. |
| SOX (Sarbanes-Oxley Act) | United States (public companies) | Financial reporting data | Requires accurate financial disclosures and strong internal controls to prevent fraud. |
| GLBA (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act) | United States (financial institutions) | Customers' financial data | Requires financial institutions to explain information-sharing practices and safeguard sensitive data. |
| Data Protection Act | United Kingdom | Personal data of UK residents | The UK's implementation of GDPR-equivalent principles for handling personal data. |

## Data Classification

1. Sensitive — data that requires protection but isn't necessarily the most critical (may overlap with other categories depending on organizational policy).
2. Confidential / R&D — proprietary research, development, or business information that would harm the organization competitively if disclosed.
3. Public — data that can be freely shared with anyone, posing no risk if disclosed.
4. Restricted — data with the tightest access controls, typically limited to a small, specifically authorized group.
5. Private — data intended for internal use only, not meant for public release but not as sensitive as restricted data.
6. Critical — data essential to the organization's core operations, where loss or compromise would cause severe impact.

## General Data Considerations — Data States

1. Data at rest — data stored on a disk or device, not actively being transmitted or processed; protected primarily through encryption and access controls.
2. Data in transit — data actively moving across a network; protected through encryption protocols like TLS or IPSec.
3. Data in use — data actively being processed in memory; harder to protect, sometimes addressed through techniques like secure enclaves.
4. Data sovereignty — the concept that data is subject to the laws of the country in which it is physically stored or collected.
5. Geolocation — using a device or system's physical location to inform access decisions or enforce data residency requirements (e.g., blocking access from certain countries, or ensuring data stays within a specific region).

## Methods to Secure Data

1. Geolocation (restriction) — restricting access to data based on the user's or device's physical location.
2. Encryption — converting data into an unreadable format that can only be reversed with the correct key.
3. Hashing — producing a one-way, fixed-length digest of data, used to verify integrity rather than to protect confidentiality.
4. Masking — replacing sensitive data with realistic but fake values, often used in non-production environments.
5. Tokenization — replacing sensitive data with a non-sensitive placeholder token that has no exploitable value on its own.
6. Obfuscation — disguising data so its true meaning isn't obvious:
   - XOR — a simple reversible bitwise operation sometimes used as a basic (weak) obfuscation technique.
   - ROT13 — a simple letter-substitution cipher that shifts each letter 13 places; easily reversible and used more for casual obfuscation than real security.
7. Segmentation — isolating sensitive data in its own network zone or system, separate from the rest of the environment.
8. Permission restrictions — limiting who can read, write, or modify data based on least privilege.
