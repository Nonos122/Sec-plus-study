# 2.1 Notes

## Threat Actors

1. Nation-state — a government-sponsored actor with significant resources, often behind Advanced Persistent Threats (APTs); focused on long-term, stealthy espionage or sabotage.
2. Unskilled attacker — sometimes called a "script kiddie"; has little technical skill and relies on pre-built tools or scripts written by others.
3. Hacktivist — motivated by political or social causes rather than money; attacks or defaces targets to make a statement.
4. Insider threat — a current or former employee, contractor, or partner who misuses their legitimate access to cause harm, whether intentionally or by accident.
5. Organized crime — a group of financially motivated criminals, often well-funded and operating like a business (e.g., ransomware gangs).
6. Shadow IT — technology (apps, devices, cloud services) used within an organization without the knowledge or approval of the IT department, creating unmanaged risk.

## Attributes of Actors

1. Internal/external — whether the actor operates from inside the organization (with existing access) or from outside it.
2. Resources/funding — how much money, staffing, and infrastructure the actor has available to carry out an attack.
3. Sophistication/capability — the actor's technical skill level, ranging from unskilled attackers using off-the-shelf tools to nation-states developing custom zero-day exploits.

## Motivations

1. Data exfiltration — stealing sensitive data from an organization.
2. Espionage — covertly gathering confidential or classified information, often for a government or competitor.
3. Service disruption — intentionally interrupting or degrading a system or service (e.g., DDoS).
4. Blackmail — threatening to release sensitive information or cause harm unless a demand (often payment) is met.
5. Financial gain — seeking direct monetary benefit from the attack, such as through fraud or ransomware.
6. Philosophical/political beliefs — acting in support of an ideology or cause (typical of hacktivists).
7. Ethical — so-called "white hat" motivations, such as responsible disclosure of vulnerabilities to improve security.
8. Revenge — acting out of a personal grievance, often seen in insider threat cases.
9. Disruption — causing chaos or damage for its own sake, without a clear financial or political goal.
10. War — attacks carried out as part of a nation-state conflict, targeting military, government, or critical infrastructure systems.
