# 2.2 Notes

## Message-Based

1. Email — the most common vector for phishing and malicious attachments/links.
2. Short Message Service (SMS) — text messages used to deliver phishing links or malicious content (smishing).
3. Instant message (IM) — chat platforms (e.g., Slack, WhatsApp) used to deliver malicious links or impersonate a trusted contact.

## Image-Based

Malicious code or hidden data embedded within an image file (e.g., steganography, or an image crafted to exploit a flaw in image-processing software).

## File-Based

Malware or malicious payloads delivered through seemingly normal files, such as infected documents, PDFs, or executables disguised as legitimate attachments.

## Voice Call

Phone-based social engineering (vishing) where an attacker impersonates a trusted source (IT support, a bank, an executive) to extract information or manipulate the victim.

## Removable Device

USB drives or other portable media used to introduce malware directly onto a system, bypassing network-based defenses (e.g., a dropped USB drive an employee plugs in out of curiosity).

## Vulnerable Software

Software with unpatched or unknown flaws that can be exploited. Identified through vulnerability scanning:

1. Client-based scanning — requires an agent installed on the target system to scan it from the inside, giving deeper visibility.
2. Agentless scanning — scans a system remotely without installing any software on it, relying on network access and credentials.

## Unsupported Systems and Applications

Systems or applications that are past end-of-life and no longer receive security patches from the vendor, leaving known vulnerabilities permanently unaddressed.

## Unsecure Networks

1. Wireless network — networks with weak or no encryption, making traffic easy to intercept.
2. Wired network — physical network connections that lack proper segmentation or access control.
3. Bluetooth — short-range wireless connections that can be exploited if not properly secured (e.g., bluejacking, bluesnarfing).

## Open Service Ports

Network ports left open unnecessarily on a system, expanding the attack surface and giving attackers more potential entry points to probe and exploit.

## Default Credentials

Usernames and passwords that ship with hardware or software and are never changed, giving attackers an easy, well-known way to gain access.

## Supply Chain

Risk introduced through third parties involved in delivering products or services:

1. Managed Service Providers (MSPs) — third-party companies given privileged access to manage IT systems, which can become a single point of compromise for all their clients.
2. Vendors — companies that supply hardware, software, or services that may introduce vulnerabilities into your environment.
3. Suppliers — organizations providing components or materials that could be compromised before reaching you (e.g., counterfeit or tampered hardware).

## Human Vectors / Social Engineering

1. Phishing — fraudulent emails designed to trick a victim into revealing information or clicking a malicious link.
2. Spear phishing — a targeted phishing attack aimed at a specific individual or organization, using personalized details to appear more convincing.
3. Smishing — phishing carried out via SMS text message.
4. Misinformation — spreading false information to deceive or manipulate, sometimes as part of a broader social engineering or influence campaign.
5. Impersonation — pretending to be someone else (a coworker, vendor, or authority figure) to gain trust or access.
6. Pretexting — creating a fabricated scenario or false identity to manipulate a victim into giving up information or access.
7. Watering hole — compromising a website the target is known to visit, so the attack reaches them without directly targeting them.
8. Brand impersonation — imitating a trusted, well-known brand (logo, email format, domain) to trick victims into trusting a malicious message or site.
9. Typosquatting — registering domains with common misspellings of legitimate sites to catch users who mistype a URL.
