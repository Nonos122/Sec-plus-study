# 5.6 Notes

## Types of Anomalous Behavior

1. Risky — behavior that increases the chance of a security incident, even without malicious intent (e.g., using weak passwords, ignoring MFA prompts, connecting to unsecured Wi-Fi).
2. Unexpected — activity that deviates from a user's normal pattern (e.g., logging in at 3 a.m. or accessing systems they've never used before), which may indicate a compromised account.
3. Unintentional — mistakes made without malicious intent (e.g., accidentally emailing sensitive data to the wrong recipient), still requiring detection and response even though there's no bad intent behind it.

## User Guidance and Training Methods

1. Policy/handbooks — formal, written documentation of expected behavior and security requirements that employees are trained on and can reference.
2. Situational awareness — training employees to stay alert to their surroundings and recognize signs of a potential security issue in the moment.
3. Insider threat — training focused on recognizing and reporting suspicious behavior from colleagues, and understanding the consequences of misusing legitimate access.
4. Password management — guidance on creating strong passwords, using password managers, and avoiding reuse across accounts.
5. Removable media and cables — training on the risks of unknown USB drives/cables (e.g., malware delivery, "juice jacking") and proper handling procedures.
6. Social engineering — training to help employees recognize phishing, pretexting, and other manipulation tactics before falling for them.
7. Hybrid/remote work environments — guidance specific to securing home networks, personal devices, and public Wi-Fi use when working outside a traditional office.

## Reporting and Monitoring

1. Initial — the first, baseline round of security awareness training typically delivered when an employee is onboarded.
2. Recurring — ongoing, periodic refresher training to keep security awareness current as threats evolve.
3. Development — creating and updating training content and materials based on new threats, incidents, or lessons learned.
4. Execution — actually delivering the training program and tracking employee completion/participation.
