# 5.4 Notes

## Compliance Reporting

1. Internal monitoring — the organization's own teams regularly check systems and processes against compliance requirements, catching issues before an external party finds them.
2. External monitoring — outside auditors, regulators, or assessors review the organization's compliance status, providing an independent, often legally required verification.

## Consequences of Non-Compliance

1. Fines — monetary penalties imposed by regulators for failing to meet compliance requirements.
2. Sanctions — formal restrictions or penalties beyond fines, such as being barred from certain business activities.
3. Reputational damage — loss of trust from customers, partners, or the public after a compliance failure becomes known.
4. Loss of license — losing the legal authorization to operate in a particular industry or jurisdiction.
5. Contractual impacts — breaching compliance requirements can violate contract terms, leading to penalties, termination, or loss of business relationships.

## Compliance Monitoring

1. Due diligence/care — consistently taking reasonable, documented steps to meet compliance obligations, demonstrating the organization is acting responsibly.
2. Attestation and acknowledgment — formally certifying (often signed by an executive) that the organization meets specific compliance requirements at a given point in time.
3. Internal and external — compliance monitoring draws on both self-assessment (internal) and independent verification (external) to get a complete picture.
4. Automation — using tools to continuously and automatically check systems against compliance requirements, rather than relying solely on periodic manual review.

## Privacy

1. Legal implications — privacy obligations vary depending on jurisdiction and must be tracked at multiple levels:
   - Local/regional — requirements specific to a city, state, or region.
   - National — requirements imposed at the country level.
   - Global — requirements spanning multiple countries (e.g., GDPR for anyone handling EU residents' data).
2. Data subject — the individual whose personal data is being collected or processed.
3. Controller — the entity that decides why and how personal data is processed.
4. Processor — the entity that handles personal data on the controller's behalf, following the controller's instructions.
5. Ownership — establishing who within the organization is accountable for a given set of personal data and its protection.
6. Data inventory — a maintained record of what personal data the organization collects, where it's stored, and how it flows through systems — a prerequisite for meeting most privacy regulations.
7. Data retention — policies defining how long personal data can be kept before it must be securely deleted.
8. Right to be forgotten — a legal right (notably under GDPR) allowing an individual to request that their personal data be deleted by an organization holding it.
