# 5.5 Notes

## Attestation

A formal declaration, often signed by an executive or auditor, confirming that a specific security or compliance requirement has been met — providing accountability and a documented record that can be relied on by regulators, partners, or leadership.

## Internal Audits

Audits performed by the organization's own staff to evaluate compliance and control effectiveness before an external party does.

1. Compliance — checking whether the organization is meeting its own policies and relevant external requirements.
2. Audit committee — a group (often reporting to the board) responsible for overseeing the audit process and ensuring findings are addressed.
3. Self-assessments — teams evaluate their own compliance and controls, often as a first step before a more formal internal or external audit.

## External Audits

Audits performed by parties outside the organization, providing independent verification.

1. Regulatory audits — conducted by a government or regulatory body to verify compliance with specific laws or regulations.
2. Examinations — formal reviews (often by regulators or industry bodies) assessing specific practices or controls in detail.
3. Independent third-party audits — conducted by an outside firm (not a regulator) to provide an unbiased assessment of security or compliance, often resulting in a report like a SOC 2.

## Pen Testing

1. Physical — testing physical security controls (badge access, locks, guard responsiveness) by attempting to gain unauthorized physical entry.
2. Offensive — actively simulating attacker techniques to find and exploit weaknesses (red team activity).
3. Defensive — focused on detecting and responding to the offensive team's activity, improving monitoring and response capability (blue team activity).
4. Integrated — offensive and defensive teams work together and share information during the engagement (purple team activity), maximizing the learning value for both sides.
5. Known environment (white box) — testers are given full knowledge of the target's systems, architecture, and source code ahead of time.
6. Partially known environment (gray box) — testers are given some information (e.g., network diagrams or limited credentials) but not full access.
7. Unknown environment (black box) — testers are given no prior knowledge, simulating a real external attacker's perspective. 
8. Reconnaissance — the information-gathering phase before actively testing a target.
   - Passive — gathering information without directly interacting with the target (e.g., searching public records, OSINT), making it harder for the target to detect.
   - Active — directly interacting with the target (e.g., port scanning) to gather information, which carries a higher chance of detection.
