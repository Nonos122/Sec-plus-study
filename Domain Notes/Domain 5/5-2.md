# 5.2 Notes

## Risk Identification

The process of discovering and documenting potential risks to the organization — vulnerabilities, threats, and the assets they could affect — before they can be assessed and managed. This is the first step in the risk management process; you can't manage a risk you haven't identified.

## Risk Assessment

Evaluating identified risks to understand their likelihood and potential impact. Can be performed on different schedules:

1. Ad hoc risk assessment — performed as needed, in response to a specific trigger (e.g., a new system deployment), rather than on a set schedule.
2. Recurring risk assessment — performed at regular, defined intervals (e.g., annually) as part of an ongoing program.
3. One-time risk assessment — performed a single time, often for a specific project or decision, without an expectation of repeating it.
4. Continuous risk assessment — an ongoing, real-time process of evaluating risk as conditions change, rather than at fixed points in time.

## Risk Analysis

Digging into the details of identified risks to quantify or characterize them:

1. Qualitative risk analysis — uses subjective ratings like High/Medium/Low to describe risk, useful when precise numbers aren't available or necessary.
2. Quantitative risk analysis — assigns numeric/dollar values to risk, allowing more precise cost-benefit comparisons.
3. SLE (Single Loss Expectancy) — the expected monetary loss from a single occurrence of a risk event; calculated as Asset Value x Exposure Factor.
4. ALE (Annualized Loss Expectancy) — the expected monetary loss from a risk over a full year; calculated as SLE x ARO.
5. ARO (Annualized Rate of Occurrence) — how many times per year a risk event is expected to occur.
6. Probability — the statistical chance that a risk event will occur.
7. Likelihood — a broader, sometimes more qualitative way of describing how probable a risk event is.
8. Exposure factor — the percentage of an asset's value that would be lost if the risk event occurred.
9. Impact — the overall effect (financial, operational, reputational) a risk event would have if it occurred.

## Risk Register

A document that tracks identified risks, their assessment, and their treatment status across the organization.

1. Key risk indicators (KRIs) — measurable metrics that provide early warning signs that a risk is increasing.
2. Risk owners — the individual accountable for managing and responding to a specific risk.
3. Risk threshold — the level of risk exposure at which action must be taken (as opposed to risk tolerance, which is the broader acceptable range).

## Risk Tolerance

The specific, defined level of risk an organization is willing to accept for a given risk or decision, used to determine whether further mitigation is required.

## Risk Appetite

The organization's general attitude toward risk-taking overall, which shapes decisions across the board:

1. Expansionary — willing to accept higher risk in pursuit of growth or opportunity.
2. Conservative — prefers to minimize risk, even at the cost of slower growth or missed opportunities.
3. Neutral — balances risk and opportunity without strongly favoring either direction.

## Risk Management Strategies

1. Transfer — shifting the risk to a third party, such as through insurance or outsourcing.
2. Accept — acknowledging the risk and choosing not to take further action, usually because the cost of mitigation exceeds the potential impact.
   - Exemption — a formal, approved decision to not comply with a specific policy/control for a valid business reason.
   - Exception — a temporary, approved deviation from policy, typically with a plan to come back into compliance later.
3. Avoid — eliminating the risk entirely, often by discontinuing the activity or system that creates it.
4. Mitigate — reducing the likelihood or impact of the risk through controls, without eliminating it entirely.

## Risk Reporting

Communicating identified risks, their assessment, and their treatment status to relevant stakeholders (management, board, regulators) in a clear, actionable way, supporting informed decision-making at the appropriate level of the organization.

## Business Impact Analysis (BIA)

Identifies critical business functions, their dependencies, and the impact of their disruption, to prioritize recovery efforts.

1. RTO (Recovery Time Objective) — the maximum acceptable amount of time a system or process can be down before recovery.
2. RPO (Recovery Point Objective) — the maximum acceptable amount of data loss, measured in time, before recovery.
3. MTTR (Mean Time to Repair/Recover) — the average time it takes to repair a failed system and restore it to operation.
4. MTBF (Mean Time Between Failures) — the average time a system operates before it fails, used as a measure of reliability.
