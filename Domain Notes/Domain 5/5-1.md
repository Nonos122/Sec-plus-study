# 5.1 Notes

## Guidelines

Recommended, non-mandatory best practices that support an organization's policies and standards — they suggest a good way to do something without legally or contractually requiring it, giving flexibility where a strict rule isn't necessary.

## Policies

1. AUP (Acceptable Use Policy) — defines what employees are and aren't allowed to do with company systems, networks, and devices.
2. Information security policies — the high-level, overarching rules defining how the organization protects its data and systems.
3. Business continuity — policy defining how the organization keeps critical operations running during and after a disruption.
4. Disaster recovery — policy defining how systems and data are restored after a major outage or disaster (works alongside business continuity, but focuses specifically on IT/technical recovery).
5. Incident response — policy defining how the organization detects, responds to, and recovers from security incidents.
6. SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle) — policy defining how security is built into each phase of software development, from design through deployment and maintenance.
7. Change management — policy defining the formal, documented process required before changes are made to systems, reducing the risk of unplanned outages or security gaps.


## Standards

1. ISO 27001 — the international standard for establishing, implementing, and maintaining an Information Security Management System (ISMS).
2. ISO 27002 — provides detailed guidance and best-practice controls that support ISO 27001's requirements.
3. ISO 27701 — extends ISO 27001/27002 to cover privacy information management (PIMS).
4. ISO/IEC 27017 — provides security guidance specifically for cloud service providers and customers.
5. ISO/IEC 27018 — provides guidance for protecting personally identifiable information (PII) in public cloud environments.
6. NIST SP 800-53 — a U.S. government catalog of security and privacy controls for federal information systems, widely used as a reference even outside government.
7. FIPS (Federal Information Processing Standards) — U.S. government standards, including cryptographic requirements (e.g., FIPS 140-2/140-3), that federal systems and vendors must meet.
8. PCI DSS (Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard) — a standard defining security requirements for any organization that stores, processes, or transmits credit card data.

## Password Standards

1. Hashing — converting a password into a fixed-length, one-way digest so the plaintext password is never stored directly.
2. Salting — adding random data to a password before hashing so identical passwords produce different hashes, defeating rainbow table attacks.
3. Encryption — protecting password data (or the systems storing it) using reversible cryptographic protection where applicable, distinct from the one-way hashing used for the passwords themselves.
4. Password reset — a defined, secure process for a user to regain access if they forget their password, without weakening security (e.g., requiring identity verification).
5. Password managers — approved tools for generating and storing strong, unique passwords, reducing password reuse across accounts.

## Access Control Standards

1. Authentication protocols — standardized methods (e.g., Kerberos, RADIUS, EAP) used to verify identity consistently across systems.
2. Least privilege — granting users and systems only the minimum access necessary to perform their function.
3. Access control type — the specific model used (MAC, DAC, RBAC, ABAC) to govern how access decisions are made.
4. User identity — ensuring each user has a unique, verifiable identity tied to their account and activity.
5. MFA (Multifactor Authentication) — requiring more than one form of verification before granting access.
6. PAM (Privileged Access Management) — tools and practices for securing and monitoring accounts with elevated privileges.
7. Audit trail — a documented record of user and system activity, supporting accountability and investigation.
8. Conditional access policy — dynamically adjusting access requirements based on context (device compliance, location, risk level) rather than applying the same rule to every login.

## Physical Security Standards

Defined requirements for protecting physical access to facilities and equipment — covering areas like badge access, visitor management, security cameras, environmental controls, and secure disposal of hardware — ensuring physical protections meet the same rigor as technical/logical controls.

## Procedures

1. Change management — the documented, step-by-step process followed to review, approve, and implement changes safely.
2. Onboarding — the documented process for provisioning a new employee's accounts, access, equipment, and required training.
3. Offboarding — the documented process for revoking access, retrieving equipment, and disabling accounts when an employee leaves or changes roles.
4. Playbooks — step-by-step, repeatable procedures for handling specific types of security events consistently (often used in incident response or SOAR automation).

## External Considerations

1. Regulatory — requirements imposed by regulatory bodies that carry legal weight and enforcement.
2. Legal — broader legal obligations the organization must meet, including contracts and liability concerns.
3. Industry — standards and expectations specific to the organization's sector (e.g., PCI DSS for retail/payments).
4. Local/regional — requirements that apply only within a specific city, state, or region.
5. National — requirements imposed at the country level.
6. Global — requirements that span multiple countries or apply to organizations operating internationally (e.g., GDPR).

## Monitoring and Revision

1. Regular audits and assessments — periodically reviewing policies, controls, and compliance status to confirm they're still effective and being followed.
2. Policy and procedure revisions — updating documentation as the organization, technology, or threat landscape changes.
3. Employee training — keeping staff up to date on current policies and procedures, not just training once at hire.
4. Legal changes — tracking new or updated laws/regulations that may require policy updates.
5. Cyclical and proactive approach — treating governance as an ongoing, repeating process rather than a one-time project, staying ahead of changes rather than reacting after the fact.

## Types of Governance Structures

1. Boards — a governing body (e.g., board of directors) providing high-level oversight and accountability for organizational risk and security posture.
2. Committees — smaller, often specialized groups (e.g., a security steering committee) that manage specific governance areas in more detail.
3. Government entities — external regulatory or legal bodies that impose requirements the organization's governance must account for.
4. Centralized/decentralized governance — centralized governance consolidates decision-making in one group for consistency; decentralized governance distributes decision-making across business units for flexibility, at the cost of consistency.

## Roles and Responsibilities for Systems and Data

1. Data owner — accountable for the data and its classification; typically a business leader responsible for deciding how the data should be protected.
2. Data controller — decides why and how personal data is processed (a term especially relevant under GDPR).
3. Data processor — handles data on the controller's behalf, following the controller's instructions, without independently deciding how it's used.
4. Data custodian — handles the technical, day-to-day storage, backup, and security controls protecting the data.
5. Data steward — manages data quality and ensures policies around data use are followed in practice.
