# 4.8 Notes

## Process (Incident Response Lifecycle)

1. Preparation — establishing plans, tools, training, and roles ahead of time so the team is ready before an incident occurs.
2. Detection — identifying that a security event has actually happened, through monitoring, alerts, or reports.
3. Analysis — investigating the detected event to understand its scope, cause, and severity.
4. Containment — isolating affected systems to stop the incident from spreading further.
5. Eradication — removing the root cause of the incident (malware, unauthorized access, vulnerability) from the environment.
6. Recovery — restoring affected systems to normal, verified-clean operation.
7. Lessons learned — reviewing the incident afterward to identify what worked, what didn't, and how to improve for next time.

## Training

Ensuring incident responders and relevant staff know their roles, procedures, and tools ahead of time, so they can act quickly and correctly during a real incident rather than improvising under pressure.

## Testing

1. Tabletop exercise — a discussion-based session where stakeholders talk through a simulated incident scenario, without touching real systems, to validate the response plan.
2. Simulation — a more realistic, hands-on exercise that mimics an actual incident (sometimes unannounced) to test how systems and people actually perform in practice.

## Root Cause Analysis

The process of digging past the immediate symptoms of an incident to identify the true underlying cause, so the fix addresses why it happened rather than just the visible effect — reducing the likelihood the same incident recurs.

## Threat Hunting

A proactive activity where analysts actively search the environment for signs of compromise or malicious activity that existing automated tools and alerts haven't already caught, rather than waiting for an alert to trigger an investigation.

## Digital Forensics

The process of collecting, preserving, and analyzing evidence from systems in a way that supports investigation and, if needed, legal proceedings.

1. Legal hold — a formal notice requiring that specific data be preserved and not deleted or altered because it may be relevant to ongoing or anticipated litigation.
2. Chain of custody — a documented record of who handled evidence, when, and how, proving it wasn't altered — required for evidence to hold up legally.
3. Acquisition — the process of collecting evidence (e.g., imaging a hard drive) in a forensically sound way that doesn't alter the original data.
4. Reporting — documenting findings from the forensic investigation clearly and thoroughly, supporting both internal decisions and any legal action.
5. Preservation — ensuring evidence remains intact and unaltered from the moment it's collected through the entire investigation.
6. E-discovery — the process of identifying, collecting, and producing electronically stored information (ESI) in response to a legal request or investigation.
