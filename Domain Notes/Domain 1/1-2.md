# 1.2 Notes

## CIA Triad

1. Confidentiality — sensitive information remains hidden from anyone not authorized to see it.
2. Integrity — data remains unaltered and trustworthy from creation through use.
3. Availability — services and data are accessible when needed.

## Non-Repudiation

Trust established through authentication, digital signatures, and audit trails, so a party cannot deny having performed an action.

1. Digital signatures — cryptographic proof used to confirm identity and that a message wasn't altered.
2. Audit trails — records of actions kept for accountability and investigation.
3. Access control — the combined process of identifying, authenticating, and authorizing a user.

## AAA

1. Authentication — verifies who someone is before granting access.
2. Authorization — determines what an authenticated user is allowed to access or do.
3. Accounting — captures details of activity, such as username, timestamps, and IP address, for later review.

### AAA Protocols

- **RADIUS** (Remote Authentication Dial-In User Service) — a client/server protocol that centralizes authentication, authorization, and accounting through a central server; commonly used for network and Wi-Fi access.
- **Diameter** — the successor to RADIUS, built to work with more modern hardware and support additional authentication methods.
- **TACACS+** (Terminal Access Controller Access-Control System Plus) — a Cisco-developed protocol used to grant or deny access, mainly for administrative control of network devices; separates authentication, authorization, and accounting into distinct processes.

### Authorization Models

- **Mandatory Access Control (MAC)** — access is controlled centrally using security labels/classifications; users can't change permissions themselves.
- **Discretionary Access Control (DAC)** — the resource owner decides who can access it.
- **Role-Based Access Control (RBAC)** — access is granted based on a user's assigned role.
- **Rule-Based Access Control** — access is granted or denied based on a defined set of rules (e.g., firewall ACLs).
- **Attribute-Based Access Control (ABAC)** — access decisions consider multiple attributes together (user, resource, environment, time, device).

## Gap Analysis

The process of comparing an organization's current security posture against a desired target state (a framework, regulation, or best-practice baseline) to identify where controls, processes, or policies fall short. The result is typically a prioritized list of gaps to remediate, feeding into a broader risk management or compliance program.

## Zero Trust

A security model built on the idea of "never trust, always verify" — no user, device, or system is trusted by default, regardless of whether it's inside or outside the traditional network perimeter. Every request is authenticated, authorized, and continuously validated.

Zero Trust architecture is generally broken into two planes:

- **Control plane** — makes the policy decisions. Key components include the **policy engine** (evaluates a request against policy and threat intelligence), the **policy administrator** (establishes or shuts down the communication path based on the engine's decision), and overall **adjudication** of whether access should be granted.
- **Data plane** — carries out the decision made by the control plane. The **policy enforcement point (PEP)** sits between the subject (user/device) and the resource, actually allowing, monitoring, or terminating the connection.

## Physical Security

Controls that protect physical access to facilities, hardware, and infrastructure:

1. Bollards — sturdy posts that prevent vehicles from ramming into a building or restricted area.
2. Access control vestibules (mantraps) — a small enclosed space with two interlocking doors, allowing only one verified person through at a time.
3. Fencing — a physical barrier marking and protecting a facility's perimeter.
4. Video surveillance (CCTV) — cameras used to monitor and record activity for deterrence and investigation.
5. Security guards — visible deterrence with the ability to actively respond to incidents.
6. Access badges — physical or smart cards used to authenticate identity and log entry/exit.
7. Lighting — deters intruders and improves the effectiveness of cameras and guards.
8. Sensors — detect unauthorized activity, including:
   - Infrared sensors — detect body heat/motion.
   - Pressure sensors — detect weight or movement on a surface.
   - Microwave sensors — detect motion using microwave pulses, effective over larger areas.
   - Ultrasonic sensors — detect motion through sound wave disruption.

## Deception and Disruption Technology

Techniques used to mislead, delay, or gather intelligence on attackers rather than simply blocking them:

1. Honeypot — a single decoy system designed to look like a legitimate target, luring attackers away from real assets while their behavior is studied.
2. Honeynet — a full decoy network of honeypots, simulating a more realistic environment to observe more sophisticated attacker behavior.
3. Honeyfile — a fake file (e.g., "passwords.xlsx") planted to detect when an attacker accesses or exfiltrates it, triggering an alert.
4. Honeytoken — a fake piece of data (credential, API key, database record) seeded into a system; any use of it signals unauthorized access, since a legitimate user would have no reason to touch it.
