## Change Management

**CAB (Change Advisory Board)** — a group responsible for reviewing and approving changes that could affect company security, ensuring changes are made in a controlled, documented way rather than ad hoc.

1. **Approval process** — a formal review and sign-off required before a change can be implemented.
2. **Ownership** — a named individual or team accountable for the change from planning through completion.
3. **Stakeholders** — the people and groups affected by or interested in the change:

   | Stakeholder | Role in the change |
   |---|---|
   | Employees | Impacted by day-to-day changes to systems/processes they use; may need training or notice. |
   | Suppliers | May need to adjust integrations, contracts, or delivery schedules affected by the change. |
   | Creditors/Investors | Care about changes that affect financial systems, risk exposure, or business continuity. |
   | Government/Regulators | Require that changes stay compliant with applicable laws and regulations. |

4. **Impact analysis** — assessing what systems, users, or processes will be affected and how significant that effect will be.
5. **Test results** — evidence that the change was validated in a test environment before deployment.
6. **Backout plan** — a documented procedure to revert the change if something goes wrong.
7. **Maintenance window** — a scheduled time frame for implementing the change, chosen to minimize disruption.
8. **Standard operating procedure (SOP)** — the documented, repeatable steps used to carry out the change consistently.

## Technical Implications

1. Allow list — explicitly permits only approved applications, IPs, or actions; everything else is blocked by default.
2. Deny list — explicitly blocks known-bad applications, IPs, or actions; everything else is allowed by default.
3. Restricted activities — actions limited or disabled during a change to reduce risk (e.g., no other changes allowed during the window).
4. Downtime — planned or unplanned unavailability of a system while a change is applied.
5. Service restart — a service must be restarted for a change to take effect, temporarily interrupting it.
6. Application restart — similar to a service restart, but at the application level.
7. Legacy applications — older applications that may not support modern security standards, complicating changes.
8. Dependencies — other systems, services, or applications that rely on the one being changed, which must be accounted for to avoid breaking something downstream.

## Documentation

1. Updating diagrams — network, system, and data flow diagrams must reflect the change once it's implemented.
2. Updating policies — security policies and procedures must be revised if the change affects how systems are secured or used.

## Version Control

A system for tracking changes to code, configurations, and documentation over time, allowing teams to:

- Record who changed what, when, and why (via commit history).
- Roll back to a previous known-good state if a change causes problems.
- Work collaboratively without overwriting each other's changes (via branching and merging).
- Maintain an audit trail that supports accountability and non-repudiation, tying directly back into change management and the backout plan.

Common tools: Git (with platforms like GitHub, GitLab, or Bitbucket).
